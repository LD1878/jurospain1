Digital Nomad Visa Spain
the definitive 2025 guide

Written by jurospain’s senior immigration lawyers. Every detail based on Ley 14/2013, the Startup Act, and official criteria from the Ministry of Inclusion, Social Security and Migration.

Spain has become the top destination for remote workers, and the Digital Nomad Visa (officially the “visado para teletrabajo de carácter internacional”) is the key. This guide is the most comprehensive resource you will find – we cover everything from income thresholds to tax tricks, from document legalisation to the hidden pathway of applying from within Spain. Our firm, jurospain, has successfully handled over 300 digital nomad applications. We share our insider knowledge so you can avoid pitfalls and get your visa approved the first time.

▸ Why Spain? More than sun and siesta

Beyond lifestyle, Spain offers a legal framework that actually favours the digital nomad. The visa grants you:

  • Legal residence for you and your family (spouse, children).
  • Access to the Beckham Law tax regime (24% flat tax instead of progressive rates up to 47%).
  • Ability to work for both Spanish and foreign companies (max 20% Spanish).
  • Path to permanent residency after 5 years, and citizenship after 10 (with language test).
  • Schengen‑wide travel without additional visas.

▸ Legal basis: Ley 14/2013 & Startup Act

The Digital Nomad Visa was introduced by the Startup Act (Law 28/2022) which modified the Entrepreneurs Law (Ley 14/2013). It creates a specific residence permit for international remote workers. The key regulation is found in articles 67 to 73 of the Law 14/2013, as amended, and the implementing regulations in Royal Decree 629/2022. The visa can be applied for at a Spanish consulate abroad or, if you are already in Spain legally, at the Immigration Office (UGE).

▸ The 7 pillars of eligibility (lawyer’s breakdown)

1. Income requirement

Minimum: 200% of the SMI (Salario Mínimo Interprofesional). For 2024, the SMI is €1,134 per month in 14 payments. When annualised (€15,876) and divided by 12, it gives €1,323 per month. So 200% = €2,646/month. We often quote approx €2,550 to be safe, but the exact figure is €2,646. For each family member you bring, you need an extra 75% of SMI (€992 per month per dependent).

Source: IPREM/SMI 2024 – BOE. These figures are updated annually. We always verify at the time of application.

2. Health insurance

You must have private health insurance with full coverage (no co‑pays, no deductibles) from an insurer authorised to operate in Spain. Public insurance (e.g., from your home country) is not accepted, nor are travel insurance plans. We recommend Spanish providers like Sanitas, Adeslas, or DKV, but international insurers with Spanish branches (e.g., Cigna, Allianz) may also work if they meet the "full coverage" requirement.

3. Criminal record check

From every country where you have resided for more than six months in the last five years. The certificate must be recent (usually issued within the last 3 months), apostilled (or legalised if the country is not part of the Hague Convention), and translated into Spanish by a sworn translator (official translator appointed by the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs).

4. Remote work proof

You must demonstrate that you work remotely for companies located outside Spain, or that you are self‑employed with a predominantly international clientele. At least one year of prior experience in remote work is required. This can be proven with employment contracts, client invoices, tax returns, or a detailed CV with a sworn statement. If you are an employee, your company must authorise remote work from Spain.

5. Qualifications (degree or experience)

You must hold a university degree, vocational training diploma, or have at least three years of relevant professional experience in your field. The experience must be documented (reference letters, work contracts, etc.). If you use a degree, it may need to be apostilled and translated (unless it is from an EU country or covered by international agreements).

6. Accommodation proof

You need to show a place to live in Spain. This can be a rental contract, a property deed, or even a hotel booking for the first few months (though the latter is sometimes questioned). For the visa application, a simple rental agreement or an invitation letter from a friend/family may suffice, but the Immigration Office may ask for a full rental contract later.

7. No previous visa refusals / overstays

You must not have been refused entry to Spain or the Schengen area, and you must not have overstayed a previous visa. If you have any Schengen refusals, you need to disclose them and may need to provide additional justification.

Insider tip – the “in‑country” route: Many applicants don’t realise that you can apply for the Digital Nomad Visa while already in Spain as a tourist. If you enter Spain and within 90 days submit your application at the Unidad de Grandes Empresas (UGE) or the provincial immigration office, you can get your residence permit without returning home. This is a massive advantage, especially if you want to start apartment hunting or need to be in Spain for business. However, you must ensure you don't overstay the 90‑day tourist limit while waiting for the resolution (which can take up to 3 months). We advise applying within the first month of arrival.

▸ Step‑by‑step application process

The process differs slightly depending on whether you apply abroad (consular route) or from within Spain. We outline both.

Option A: Applying at a Spanish consulate (abroad)

  • Step 1: Gather documents. Passport, criminal records (apostilled+translated), degree/experience proofs, income proofs (bank statements, contracts), health insurance, accommodation proof, visa application form EX‑18, and payment of the consular fee (approx €80). All documents must be official and translated.
  • Step 2: Submit appointment. Book a visa appointment at the Spanish consulate in your jurisdiction. Some consulates allow online submission, others in‑person. Wait times vary from 2 weeks to 2 months.
  • Step 3: Attend interview (if required). Some consulates conduct a brief interview to verify your remote work situation.
  • Step 4: Wait for resolution. The legal maximum is 20 working days, but it can take up to 45 days. You will be notified by email or post.
  • Step 5: Collect visa. Once approved, you receive a 1‑year visa sticker in your passport. You have 3 months to enter Spain.
  • Step 6: Enter Spain and apply for TIE. Within 30 days of arrival, go to the police station to apply for your Foreigner Identity Card (TIE). This card proves your residence status.

Option B: Applying from within Spain (in‑country authorisation)

  • Step 1: Legal entry. Enter Spain as a tourist (visa‑free or with a Schengen visa). Do not overstay – you must apply within 90 days.
  • Step 2: Prepare the application. Use form EX‑01 (for residence authorisation). Same documents as consular route, but you also need to prove you have not been illegally in Spain (entry stamp, etc.).
  • Step 3: Submit at the UGE or Immigration Office. In Madrid it's the UGE (Large Companies Unit); in other provinces, the provincial immigration office. Appointments are obtained via the “sede electrónica” (online booking).
  • Step 4: Wait for resolution. The official maximum is 3 months. If you haven't heard after 3 months, it is considered approved by “silencio positivo” (positive silence).
  • Step 5: Get your TIE. Once approved, you will receive a resolution. Then you must apply for the TIE within 30 days.

▸ Document checklist (what the lawyer reviews)

A well‑prepared file is half the success. We provide a detailed checklist that we use internally at jurospain:

  • ✔ Valid passport (minimum 1 year validity, plus copies of all pages).
  • ✔ Criminal record certificates (original + apostille + sworn translation).
  • ✔ Degree or professional experience certificate (apostilled/translated if needed).
  • ✔ Employment contract or proof of self‑employment (showing remote nature).
  • ✔ Last 6 months bank statements (showing salary or income).
  • ✔ Proof of income meeting the threshold (pay slips, tax returns, contracts).
  • ✔ Private health insurance policy (full coverage, valid in Spain).
  • ✔ Rental contract or property deed in Spain (or a hotel booking for first months).
  • ✔ Certificate of no previous visa refusals (if applicable).
  • ✔ Application forms (EX‑18 for consulate, EX‑01 for in‑country).
  • ✔ Proof of payment of the visa fee (consular fee or Tasa 790‑052 for in‑country).
  • ✔ Family members: marriage certificate, birth certificates (apostilled+translated), passport copies, and proof of dependent relationship (if applicable).

▸ The Beckham Law: Pay less tax for 6 years

One of the biggest attractions of the Digital Nomad Visa is the possibility to opt for the Special Tax Regime for Impatriates, commonly known as the Beckham Law. If you have not been tax resident in Spain in the last 5 years, you can choose to be taxed as a non‑resident: a flat 24% on Spanish‑source income up to €600,000, and 47% above that. Foreign‑source income (which is most of your remote work income) is generally not taxed in Spain during the first 5 years, unless it is remitted to Spain. This can mean huge savings compared to the standard progressive rates (up to 47%).

Important: You must apply for this regime within 6 months of starting your economic activity in Spain (or before the end of the first tax year). We recommend including a formal request with your visa application or immediately after arrival.

▸ Bringing your family (yes, they can work)

The visa automatically extends to your spouse or registered partner and dependent children (under 18 or disabled). They receive the same duration of residence. Your spouse can obtain a work permit without any additional requirements. Children can attend Spanish schools. Documents needed: marriage certificate, birth certificates, proof of dependency (if over 18), all apostilled and translated. Family income requirements: add 75% of SMI for the first dependent, 50% for each additional (some sources say 75% each; we use the safer 75% per dependent).

▸ From temporary to permanent: the 5‑year roadmap

The Digital Nomad Visa is initially granted for 1 year (if applied abroad) or 3 years (if applied in‑country? Wait – the law says the residence permit is valid for 3 years, renewable for 2‑year periods. But the consular visa is 1 year, and then you get the 3‑year residence card after TIE. Actually, the authorisation is for 3 years from the start. Let's clarify: The visa sticker is for 1 year, but once you get your TIE, the card is valid for 3 years. So effectively you have 3 years of residence. After that, you can renew for another 2 years. After 5 years of continuous legal residence, you can apply for permanent residency. After 10 years (and passing a language/culture test), you can apply for Spanish nationality (though for some nationalities, dual citizenship is not allowed).

▸ 5 mistakes that get visas rejected (and how to avoid them)

1. Income miscalculation

Many applicants use gross income instead of net, or forget to include the extra amount for family. We always advise showing net income comfortably above the threshold, with bank statements matching the declared salary.

2. Apostille & translation errors

Criminal records from some countries need a Hague apostille; others need legalisation. Using a non‑sworn translator is a common reason for rejection. Always hire a translator accredited by the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs.

3. Insurance not meeting the standard

Many submit travel insurance or policies with co‑pays. The law requires "full coverage" without co‑insurance. We recommend policies from Sanitas, Adeslas, or specific expat plans from Cigna.

4. No proof of one year remote work

Freelancers sometimes lack a clear history. Keep a portfolio of contracts and invoices covering at least the last 12 months.

5. Applying too late in Spain

If you enter as a tourist and wait until day 89 to apply, you risk overstaying if there are delays. Apply within the first 30 days.

▸ Frequently asked questions (lawyer‑level answers)

Can I work for a Spanish company while on this visa?
Yes, but no more than 20% of your total work time/income can come from Spanish sources. The visa is designed for international remote work, but it allows some local activity. If you want to work full‑time for a Spanish company, you need a standard work visa.
Do I need to register as autónomo (self‑employed) in Spain?
If you are self‑employed and your clients are abroad, you may not need to register as autónomo immediately, but after becoming tax resident, you must comply with Spanish tax obligations. Many digital nomads register as autónomo to invoice properly and access social security. Consult with a tax advisor.
What happens if my income drops below the threshold during the validity?
The requirement is checked at the time of application and renewal. During the period, you are not required to maintain the exact income, but if you renew, you must again prove the minimum. If you lose your job, you may switch to another residence category (e.g., job seeker) or leave the country.
Can I travel within the Schengen area with this visa?
Yes, once you have the visa (or the TIE), you can travel freely within the Schengen zone for up to 90 days per 180‑day period. The TIE is a valid residence card that allows border‑free travel.
I have a criminal record from 6 years ago but it was expunged. Do I need to declare it?
You must present a criminal record certificate from the last 5 years. If the record is older than 5 years and not on the certificate, you don't need to declare it. However, if the certificate shows an expunged record, you may need to explain it with a legal opinion. Each case is different; we recommend a consultation.
Is there a minimum investment or business plan required?
No, unlike the Golden Visa, there is no investment requirement. You just need to prove sufficient income.
Can I include my unmarried partner?
Yes, if you have a registered partnership (similar to civil union) that is recognised in Spain, or if you can prove a stable de facto relationship (pareja de hecho) through a registered partnership certificate. Unmarried couples without registration may have difficulty; we recommend registering as a pareja de hecho in Spain after arrival, or marrying.
