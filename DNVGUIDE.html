1. Legal basis: Ley 14/2013 and the Startup Act

The Digital Nomad Visa was introduced by the Startup Act (Law 28/2022), which amended the Entrepreneurs Law (Ley 14/2013). The specific provisions are found in articles 67 to 73 of Law 14/2013, as well as in Royal Decree 629/2022. The visa allows non-EU nationals to reside in Spain while working remotely for companies outside Spain, or for Spanish companies up to 20% of their activity. It is a residence permit, not merely a stay permit, meaning you can eventually apply for permanent residency and citizenship.

The permit can be obtained through two distinct procedures: consular application (if you reside abroad) or in-country application (if you are already in Spain legally, e.g., as a tourist). We will detail both.

2. The seven pillars of eligibility

To qualify, you must meet all of the following requirements. We break down each one with lawyer-level precision.

Minimum Income

EUR 2,646 per month (or EUR 31,752 per year). This is 200% of Spain's SMI. For each family member, add EUR 992 per dependent. Figures update annually; we verify at application time.

Private Health Insurance

Full coverage with no co-pays, no deductibles from an insurer authorised in Spain. Travel insurance is not accepted. We recommend Sanitas, Adeslas, DKV, or Cigna/Allianz Spanish branches.

Criminal Record Check

From every country where you've resided 6+ months in the past five years. Must be recent, apostilled, and translated by a Ministry-accredited sworn translator. This requirement trips up many applicants.

Remote Work Proof

Employees need employer authorisation for remote work from Spain. Self-employed must show 80%+ income from abroad. You also need one year of prior remote work experience documented through contracts or tax returns.

Qualifications

Hold a university degree, vocational diploma, or have three years of relevant professional experience. Degrees may need apostille and translation (EU exemptions apply). Experience requires reference letters and contracts.

Accommodation Proof

Show a place to live in Spain: rental contract, property deed, or hotel booking. For the initial visa, a simple rental agreement or invitation letter may suffice. You'll need a formal contract for the TIE card later.

Clean Immigration History

No prior Schengen denials or overstays. If you have refusals on record, disclose them with justification. Immigration history is verified; transparency is essential.

Insider tip - the "in-country" route: Many applicants don't realise you can apply for the Digital Nomad Visa while already in Spain as a tourist. Enter Spain and within 90 days submit your application at the Unidad de Grandes Empresas (UGE) in Madrid or your provincial immigration office. This lets you secure housing before residence is confirmed, without returning home. Critical: apply within 30 days of arrival to avoid overstay risk during the 3-month processing window. Form EX-01, fee Tasa 790-052.

3. Step-by-step: consular vs. in-country

Choose the route that fits your situation.

Path A: Consular Application (From Abroad)

  • Document preparation. Gather all documents. Ensure every foreign public document is apostilled and translated by a Ministry-accredited translator. Make copies.
  • Complete form EX-18. Download from the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs website.
  • Pay visa fee. Approximately EUR 80 (varies by consulate). Keep receipt.
  • Book appointment. Visit your consulate's website. Wait times range from one to two months. Some accept postal submissions.
  • Interview (if required). Some consulates may verify documents in person.
  • Wait for resolution. Legal maximum is 20 working days; expect 30-45 days. Notification by email or post.
  • Collect visa. One-year visa sticker for your passport. Must enter Spain within three months.
  • Apply for TIE. Within 30 days of arrival, visit your local immigration office for fingerprinting and photo. Card arrives in 3-4 weeks.

Path B: In-Country Application (From Within Spain)

  • Enter Spain legally. Arrive as tourist (visa-free if eligible, or on Schengen visa). Get entry stamp. Must apply within 90 days.
  • Prepare application (form EX-01). Documents identical to consular route, plus proof of legal entry: passport stamp, boarding passes.
  • Pay residence fee. Tasa 790-052, approximately EUR 15-20. Verify current rates.
  • Book appointment. Use the online booking system at your provincial immigration office. Madrid uses the UGE (Unidad de Grandes Empresas).
  • Submit in person. Bring all documents and fee receipt. Receive stamped receipt (resguardo) confirming submission.
  • Wait for resolution. Legal maximum is 3 months. If no response after 3 months, approval by "silencio positivo" (positive administrative silence). Follow up proactively anyway.
  • Receive approval letter. Then apply for TIE within 30 days.
  • Get TIE. Valid for three years from approval date.

4. The ultimate document checklist

A complete, error-free file is the key to approval. This is our internal checklist for every client.

  • Passport: Valid 1+ year, copy all pages including blanks.
  • Criminal records: Original + apostille + sworn Spanish translation (all countries, last 5 years).
  • Qualifications: Degree (apostilled/translated) or reference letters, contracts, tax summaries.
  • Employment proof: Employer letter (employees) or contracts/invoices (self-employed). Must state remote nature, company, duration, salary.
  • Bank statements: Last 6 months showing salary deposits. Include tax returns if self-employed.
  • Income calculation: Spreadsheet showing monthly income exceeds threshold plus dependents.
  • Health insurance: Policy from Spanish-authorised insurer. Explicit "full coverage, no co-pays."
  • Accommodation: Rental contract, property deed, or hotel booking with length confirmation.
  • Application form: EX-18 (consular) or EX-01 (in-country), signed.
  • Visa fee receipt: Consular fee or Tasa 790-052.
  • Family documents: Marriage certificate, birth certificates (apostilled/translated), passport copies, dependency proof.
  • Proof of legal entry (in-country only): Entry stamp, boarding passes, Schengen visa.
  • Curriculum Vitae: Detailing remote work experience and qualifications.
  • Business registration (self-employed): Registration certificate, VAT number.

5. The Beckham Law: Strategic tax planning

One of the most powerful incentives of the Digital Nomad Visa is access to the Special Tax Regime for Impatriates, popularly known as the Beckham Law. If you haven't been Spanish tax resident in the past five years, you can elect to be taxed as a non-resident for up to six years.

The advantage: Spanish-source income is taxed at flat 24% (up to EUR 600,000 annually; 47% above). Crucially, your foreign-source income (the majority of remote work earnings) is generally not taxed by Spain unless remitted to a Spanish bank account. This can save thousands annually compared to standard progressive rates (up to 47%).

How to apply: File Form 149 with the Spanish tax agency (AEAT) within six months of starting economic activity in Spain, or before year-end of your first tax year. We recommend noting your intention to elect this regime in your visa application, then follow up with a tax advisor immediately upon arrival.

6. Family reunification: spouse, children, partners

Your residence extends to qualifying family members. They receive the same visa duration and can work or study freely.

Eligible dependents:

  • Spouse (marriage certificate required, apostilled + translated)
  • Registered partner (civil union or pareja de hecho certificate recognised in Spain)
  • Children under 18, or older children with disabilities (birth certificates + dependency proof)
  • Parents are not included under this visa, only spouse and children

Income requirement for family: Add EUR 992 per dependent (75% of monthly SMI). For a couple with two children: EUR 2,646 + (3 x EUR 992) = EUR 5,622 monthly.

7. Renewal and the road to permanent residency

Your initial TIE (Foreigner Identity Card) is valid for three years. Renewal requires applying 60 days before expiry (or within 90 days after, incurring penalty). You must re-verify income, insurance, and legal compliance.

After five years of continuous legal residence, you can apply for permanent residency (residencia de larga duracion). This status is indefinite and requires no renewal.

After ten years of residence and passing a language and culture exam, you can apply for Spanish nationality. Spain permits dual citizenship with most countries, but verify your home country's stance.

8. Five mistakes that lead to rejection

Income Miscalculation

Using gross instead of net, or forgetting dependent increments. Show net income well above threshold. Average variable income over 12 months.

Apostille & Translation Errors

Using non-accredited translators is a leading rejection cause. Always hire Ministry-certified sworn translators. Translation must be signed and sealed.

Insurance Deficiencies

Travel insurance or policies with co-pays are rejected. Policies must explicitly state "full coverage, zero co-pays." Verify before submitting.

Missing Remote Work History

Lack of documented 12-month history causes rejection. Keep contracts and invoices. If newly self-employed, show prior remote employment.

Overstaying Your Tourist Status

Waiting until day 89 risks overstay penalties if delays occur. Apply within 30 days. Verify entry visa validity for your nationality.

9. Frequently asked questions

Can I work for Spanish companies on this visa?
Yes, but work for Spanish entities cannot exceed 20% of your professional activity. Full-time Spanish employment requires a standard work visa (cuenta ajena).
Do I need to register as autónomo (self-employed) in Spain?
If clients are abroad, you may not need immediate registration. However, once tax resident, Spanish law requires tax compliance. Many nomads register as autónomo to invoice properly and access social security. Consult a tax advisor.
What if my income drops below the threshold during validity?
Income is verified at application and renewal only. During the permit period, you need not maintain it. Renewal requires re-verification. Inform immigration authorities of significant changes.
Can I travel within Schengen on this visa?
Yes. Once you have the visa or TIE, you enjoy visa-free Schengen travel for up to 90 days per 180-day period. The TIE is a valid residence card. Carry passport and TIE when travelling.
I have a criminal record from 6 years ago but it was expunged. Do I need to declare it?
You must present a certificate covering the last 5 years. If the record is older than 5 years and not on the certificate, you don't need to declare it. If expunged but listed, a legal opinion may help. Each case differs; consult a lawyer.
Is there a minimum investment requirement?
No. Unlike the Golden Visa, there is no investment threshold. You need only demonstrate sufficient income. This makes it one of Europe's most accessible residency routes for remote workers.
Can I include my unmarried partner?
Yes, if you have a registered civil union or pareja de hecho (domestic partnership) certificate. Unmarried couples without registration face difficulty. Register as pareja de hecho in Spain after arrival, or marry. Certificate must be apostilled and translated.
What is the difference between the visa and the TIE?
The visa is a one-year sticker in your passport allowing entry to Spain. The TIE (Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero) is the physical residence card obtained after arrival, valid for three years. The TIE is your official residence document.
How long does the entire process take?
Consular route: 1-3 months for visa approval, then 1-2 months for TIE after arrival. In-country route: up to 3 months for authorisation, then 1-2 months for TIE. Plan for 4-5 months total.

10. Your first 30 days in Spain

Once you arrive with your visa, these steps are essential:

  1. Apply for the TIE. Book a cita previa at your local immigration office. Provide fingerprints and photo. Receive receipt, card in 3-4 weeks.
  2. Register for social security (if applicable). If you plan to work for Spanish clients, consult a gestor about registering as autónomo.
  3. Open a Spanish bank account. Essential for rent, utilities, and receiving payments.
  4. Register on the padrón (municipal register). Mandatory for residents. Visit your local ayuntamiento with rental contract and passport.
  5. File Form 149 (Beckham Law). If using the special tax regime, file with AEAT within six months of starting economic activity.
