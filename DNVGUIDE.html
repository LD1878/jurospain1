Spain has emerged as Europe's premier destination for remote workers seeking legal residency, healthcare access, and a favourable tax environment. The Digital Nomad Visa—officially the visado para teletrabajo de carácter internacional—is not merely a tourist document. It grants full legal residence, pathway to permanent residency, and access to Spain's exceptional public healthcare. Unlike temporary stays, this visa positions you within Spain's legal framework.

1. The Legal Framework

The Digital Nomad Visa was introduced by the Startup Act (Law 28/2022), which amended the Entrepreneurs Law (Ley 14/2013). The operative provisions are Articles 67–73 of Law 14/2013, along with Royal Decree 629/2022. This visa grants a residence permit—not merely a stay permit—meaning you build a pathway to permanent residency and eventually citizenship.

Two distinct procedures exist:

  • ✦ Consular Application: From your home country, through the Spanish consulate.
  • ✦ In-Country Application: From within Spain, while on a tourist stay.

Each has strategic advantages we explore in detail below.

2. The Seven Pillars of Eligibility

To qualify, you must satisfy all of the following requirements. We examine each with legal precision.

1

Monthly Income Threshold

€2,646 per month (€31,752 annually). This equals 200% of Spain's SMI (Salario Mínimo Interprofesional). For dependents, add €992 per person (75% of SMI). These figures adjust annually. For a spouse plus two children, you require €5,622 monthly.

2

Health Insurance

Full private coverage with zero co-pays, zero deductibles from a Spanish-authorised insurer. Travel insurance or home-country public coverage is rejected. We recommend Sanitas, Adeslas, DKV, or international operators with Spanish branches (Cigna, Allianz). The policy must cover all risks standard to Spain's national health system.

3

Criminal Record Clearance

A clean certificate from every country where you resided 6+ months in the past five years. Certificates must be recent (within 3 months), apostilled (Hague Convention countries) or legalised (others), and translated into Spanish by a Ministry-recognised sworn translator. This requirement trips up many applicants—ensure your translator is accredited.

4

Remote Work Proof

Employees need a letter from their employer authorising remote work from Spain. Self-employed professionals must show contracts and invoices proving at least 80% of income derives from abroad. You must also document one year of prior remote work experience through contracts, tax returns, or detailed CV with supporting evidence.

5

Qualifications

Hold a university degree, vocational diploma, or demonstrate three years of relevant professional experience. Degrees may require apostille and translation (EU qualifications exempt). Experience must be documented with reference letters, contracts, and certifications. Professional credentials matter—not just any work history.

6

Accommodation

Proof of a place to live in Spain: rental contract, property deed, or hotel booking (the latter considered weak). For initial visa, a simple rental agreement or invitation letter may suffice. For the TIE card (after arrival), you will need a formal rental contract from your landlord.

7

Immigration Integrity

No prior Schengen denials, no overstays. If you have a refusal on record, disclosure with justification (e.g., administrative error) may remedy this. Immigration history is verified; transparency is essential.

The In-Country Route: Many applicants overlook this path. You can enter Spain as a tourist and apply for the Digital Nomad Visa while on soil—within 90 days. This lets you secure housing and network before your residence is confirmed. The application (Form EX-01) goes to the Unidad de Grandes Empresas (Madrid) or provincial immigration offices. Critical: apply within 30 days of arrival to avoid overstay risk during processing (which takes up to 3 months). This route grants legal residence without returning home.

3. Two Paths: Consular vs. In-Country

Path A: Consular Application (From Abroad)

  1. Prepare all documents. Ensure every foreign public document is apostilled and translated by a Ministry-accredited sworn translator. Create full copies of all pages (including blank passport pages).
  2. Complete Form EX-18. Download from the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs website. Sign and date.
  3. Pay the visa fee. Approximately €80 (varies by consulate). Retain the receipt.
  4. Schedule your consular appointment. Visit your consulate's website for available dates. Wait times range from one week to two months; some consulates accept postal submissions.
  5. Attend the interview (if required). Some consulates interview applicants to verify documents and confirm remote work legitimacy. Prepare to discuss your employment arrangement.
  6. Await resolution. The legal maximum is 20 working days; expect 30–45 days. You'll receive notification by email or post.
  7. Collect your visa. A one-year visa sticker is affixed to your passport. You must enter Spain within three months of issuance.
  8. Apply for your TIE card. Within 30 days of arrival, book an appointment at your local oficina de extranjería (immigration office). You'll be fingerprinted and photographed; the card arrives in 3–4 weeks.

Path B: In-Country Application (From Within Spain)

  1. Enter Spain legally. Arrive as a tourist (visa-free if eligible, or on a Schengen visa). Ensure you receive an entry stamp. Do not overstay; you must apply within 90 days.
  2. Prepare application (Form EX-01). Document requirements are identical to the consular route, plus proof of legal entry: passport stamp, boarding passes, visa pages.
  3. Pay the residence fee (Tasa 790-052). Approximately €15–20. Verify current rates before submitting.
  4. Book your appointment. Use the sede electrónica (online booking system) at the immigration office. In Madrid, submit to the UGE (Unidad de Grandes Empresas); elsewhere, the provincial immigration office.
  5. Submit your application in person. Bring all documents and fee receipt. You receive a stamped resguardo (receipt) confirming submission and a reference number.
  6. Wait for resolution. Maximum 3 months. If you hear nothing after 3 months, the application is approved by silencio positivo (positive administrative silence). However, do not rely on silence; follow up proactively.
  7. Receive your approval letter. Notification arrives by post or email. You then have 30 days to apply for the TIE card.
  8. Apply for your TIE. Same process: local immigration office, fingerprints, photograph. Valid for three years.

4. The Complete Document Checklist

A flawless file is the key to approval. This is our internal checklist, used for every client at JUROSpain:

  • Passport: Valid for at least one year; full copy of all pages including blanks.
  • Criminal record certificates: Original + apostille + sworn Spanish translation from every country of residence (last 5 years).
  • Qualifications: Degree diploma (apostilled/translated if non-EU) or three reference letters plus contracts documenting professional experience.
  • Employment proof: Employer letter (for employees) or contracts/invoices (for self-employed), stating remote nature, company details, duration, and salary.
  • Bank statements: Last 6 months showing regular salary deposits or income. Include tax returns if self-employed.
  • Income certification: A spreadsheet we prepare showing monthly income exceeds the threshold plus any dependents.
  • Health insurance: Policy document from Spanish-authorised insurer explicitly stating "full coverage, no co-pays."
  • Accommodation proof: Rental contract, property deed, or hotel booking with length confirmation.
  • Application form: EX-18 (consular) or EX-01 (in-country), signed and dated.
  • Visa fee receipt: Consular fee (€80) or Tasa 790-052 (€15–20).
  • Family documents (if applicable): Marriage certificate, birth certificates (apostilled/translated), passport copies, dependency proof.
  • Proof of legal entry (in-country only): Entry stamp, boarding passes, Schengen visa pages.
  • Curriculum Vitae: Detailing remote work experience and qualifications.
  • Business registration (if self-employed): Registration certificate, VAT number, incorporation documents.

5. The Beckham Law: Strategic Tax Planning

One of the visa's most powerful incentives is access to the Special Tax Regime for Impatriates—the Beckham Law. If you have not been Spanish tax resident in the prior five years, you can elect to be taxed as a non-resident for up to six years.

The advantage: Spanish-source income is taxed at a flat 24% (up to €600,000 annually; 47% above). Critically, your foreign-source income—the bulk of your remote work earnings—is generally not taxed by Spain unless remitted to a Spanish bank account. This can yield savings of thousands annually compared to standard progressive rates (which reach 47%).

How to apply: File Form 149 with the Spanish tax authority (AEAT) within six months of beginning economic activity in Spain, or before year-end of your first tax year. We recommend noting your intention to elect this regime in your visa application, then following up with a Spanish tax advisor immediately upon arrival.

Consult with a tax professional to verify eligibility and optimize your specific situation.

6. Bringing Family: Spouse, Children, Partners

Your residence extends to qualifying family members. They receive the same visa duration and can work or study freely.

Eligible dependents:

  • Spouse: Marriage certificate required (apostilled + translated).
  • Registered partner: Civil union or pareja de hecho certificate recognised in Spain.
  • Children: Under 18, or older children with disabilities (birth certificates + dependency proof).
  • Parents: Unfortunately excluded. Parents may qualify for other reunification routes once you attain permanent residency.

Income requirements for dependents: Add €992 per dependent (75% of monthly SMI). For a couple with two children: €2,646 + (3 × €992) = €5,622 monthly.

7. Renewal & the Pathway to Permanence

Your initial TIE (Foreigner Identity Card) is valid for three years. Renewal requires applying 60 days before expiry (or within 90 days after, incurring a penalty). You must re-verify that income, insurance, and legal compliance remain intact.

After five years of continuous legal residence, you become eligible for permanent residency (residencia de larga duración). This status is indefinite and requires no renewal—a significant milestone.

After ten years of residence and passing a language and culture exam, you may apply for Spanish nationality. Note that Spain permits dual citizenship with most countries, but verify your specific home country's stance.

8. Five Mistakes That Trigger Rejection

1

Income Miscalculation

Using gross instead of net income, or forgetting dependents' increments. We always demonstrate net income comfortably above the threshold with bank statements matching declared salary. For variable income, average the last 12 months.

2

Apostille & Translation Errors

Using non-accredited translators is a leading rejection cause. Always hire a translator certified by Spain's Ministry of Foreign Affairs. The translation must be signed, sealed, and dated.

3

Insurance Deficiencies

Travel insurance or policies with co-pays are rejected. Policies must explicitly state "full coverage, zero co-pays." Verify before submitting.

4

Missing One-Year Remote History

If newly self-employed, lack of documented remote work history causes rejection. Keep a portfolio of contracts and invoices spanning 12+ months. If you lack freelance history, show prior employment in a remote capacity.

5

Overstaying While In-Country

Applicants who wait until day 89 to submit risk overstay penalties if processing delays occur. Apply within 30 days of arrival. Also verify that your entry visa (if required) is valid—some nationalities require a Schengen visa.

9. Frequently Asked Questions

Can I work for Spanish companies on this visa?
Yes, but work for Spanish entities cannot exceed 20% of your professional activity. If you require full-time Spanish employment, you need a standard work visa.
Must I register as autónomo (self-employed) in Spain?
If your clients are abroad, you may not need to register immediately. However, once tax resident, Spanish law requires compliance with tax obligations. Many nomads register as autónomo to invoice properly and access social security. A Spanish tax advisor can guide your specific structure.
What if my income drops below the threshold during the visa period?
Income is verified at application and renewal. During the permit period, you are not required to maintain it, but renewal will again require proof. Significant changes should be reported to immigration authorities.
Can I travel the Schengen area on this visa?
Yes. Once you hold the visa (or TIE), you enjoy visa-free Schengen travel for up to 90 days per 180-day period. The TIE serves as your residence card for border crossings. Always carry your passport and TIE.
I have a criminal record from six years ago. Can I apply?
You must provide a clean certificate from the past five years. Older records need not be declared if they don't appear on the certificate. If an expunged record appears, a legal opinion clarifying its status may help. Each case is unique; consult a lawyer.
Is there a minimum investment required?
No. Unlike the Golden Visa, there is no investment threshold. You need only demonstrate sufficient income—making this one of Europe's most accessible residency routes for remote workers.
Can I include my unmarried partner?
Yes, if you have a registered civil union or pareja de hecho (domestic partnership) certificate. Unmarried couples without registration face complications; we recommend formalising your status in Spain after arrival, or marrying. The certificate must be apostilled and translated.
What is the difference between the visa and the TIE?
The visa is a one-year sticker in your passport allowing entry to Spain. The TIE is your official residence card, valid for three years, obtained after arrival. The TIE is proof of legal residence and your identity document in Spain.
How long does the entire process take?
Consular route: 1–3 months for visa approval; 1–2 months for TIE after arrival. In-country route: Up to 3 months for authorisation; 1–2 months for TIE. Plan for 4–5 months total from application to TIE in hand.

10. Your First 30 Days in Spain

Once you arrive with your visa, these steps are essential:

  1. Apply for your TIE. Book a cita previa at the nearest oficina de extranjería. Provide fingerprints and a photograph. You'll receive a receipt and the card in 3–4 weeks.
  2. Register for social security (if applicable). If you plan to work for Spanish clients, consult a gestor about registering as autónomo. This grants access to Spanish healthcare and social benefits.
  3. Open a Spanish bank account. Essential for rent, utilities, and receiving payments. Most banks require your TIE or a temporary receipt from the immigration office.
  4. Register on the padrón (municipal register). Mandatory for all residents. Visit your local ayuntamiento with your rental contract and passport. This is required for healthcare, voting, and many services.
  5. File Form 149 (Beckham Law election). If you intend to use the special tax regime, file with the AEAT within six months of starting economic activity. A tax advisor can prepare this.
