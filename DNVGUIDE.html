Spain Digital Nomad Visa
the ultimate 2025 guide

Written by the senior immigration lawyers at JUROSpain. Every fact checked against Ley 14/2013, the Startup Act, and official criteria from the Ministry of Inclusion, Social Security and Migration.

Spain has become the epicentre for remote workers seeking a vibrant European base. The Digital Nomad Visa (officially the “visado para teletrabajo de carácter internacional”) is your gateway. Unlike temporary tourist stays, this visa grants you legal residence, access to Spain’s exceptional healthcare, a favourable tax regime, and a path to permanent residency. In this guide, we leave no stone unturned. You will learn the exact income threshold, how to apply from your home country or from within Spain, the secret clauses that allow you to work for Spanish companies, and the tax tricks that can save you thousands. JUROSpain has handled over 400 digital nomad applications; we share our inside knowledge so you can approach the process with confidence.

1. Legal basis: Ley 14/2013 & the Startup Act

The Digital Nomad Visa was introduced by the Startup Act (Law 28/2022), which amended the Entrepreneurs Law (Ley 14/2013). The specific provisions are found in articles 67 to 73 of Law 14/2013, as well as in Royal Decree 629/2022. The visa allows non‑EU nationals to reside in Spain while working remotely for companies outside Spain, or for Spanish companies up to 20% of their activity. It is a residence permit, not merely a stay permit, meaning you can eventually apply for permanent residency and citizenship.

The permit can be obtained through two distinct procedures: consular application (if you reside abroad) or in‑country application (if you are already in Spain legally, e.g., as a tourist). We will detail both.

2. The seven pillars of eligibility

To qualify, you must meet all of the following requirements. We break down each one with lawyer‑level precision.

1. Minimum income

€2,646 per month (or €31,752 per year). This is 200% of Spain’s SMI (Salario Mínimo Interprofesional) for 2024. The SMI is set at €1,134 per month in 14 payments, which annualises to €15,876. Divided by 12, the monthly reference is €1,323. 200% = €2,646. For each family member you bring, you need an extra 75% of the monthly SMI (€992 per dependent). These figures are updated annually; we always verify at the time of application.

2. Private health insurance

Full coverage with no co‑pays, no deductibles, from an insurer authorised to operate in Spain. Travel insurance or public insurance from your home country is not accepted. We recommend Spanish insurers (Sanitas, Adeslas, DKV) or international insurers with a Spanish branch (Cigna, Allianz). The policy must cover all risks typically covered by the Spanish national health system.

3. Criminal record check

From every country where you have resided for more than six months in the last five years. The certificate must be recent (usually issued within the last three months), apostilled (if the country is party to the Hague Convention) or legalised (if not), and translated into Spanish by a sworn translator officially recognised by the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs. This is a common stumbling block – ensure your translator is accredited.

4. Remote work proof

You must demonstrate that you work remotely for companies or clients outside Spain. Employees need a letter from their employer authorising remote work from Spain. Self‑employed professionals must show contracts and invoices proving that at least 80% of their income comes from abroad. Additionally, you need at least one year of prior remote work experience – documented through contracts, tax returns, or a detailed CV with supporting evidence.

5. Qualifications (degree or experience)

You must hold a university degree, vocational training diploma, or have at least three years of relevant professional experience in your field. If using a degree, it may need to be apostilled and translated (unless it is from an EU country). Experience must be documented with reference letters, work contracts, and any relevant certifications.

6. Accommodation proof

You need to show a place to live in Spain. This can be a rental contract, a property deed, or even a hotel booking for the first few months (though the latter may be considered weak). For the initial visa, a simple rental agreement or an invitation letter from a friend/family member may suffice. After arrival, you will need a full rental contract for the TIE application.

7. Clean immigration history

You must not have been refused entry to Spain or the Schengen area, and you must not have overstayed a previous visa. If you have any refusals, you need to disclose them and may need to provide additional justification (e.g., the refusal was based on a misunderstanding).

Insider tip – the “in‑country” route: Many applicants don’t realise that you can apply for the Digital Nomad Visa while already in Spain as a tourist. If you enter Spain and within 90 days submit your application at the Unidad de Grandes Empresas (UGE) in Madrid or the provincial immigration office, you can get your residence permit without returning home. This is especially useful if you want to secure housing or start networking. However, you must not overstay the 90‑day tourist limit while waiting for the resolution (which can take up to 3 months). We advise applying within the first month of arrival to create a buffer. The application form is EX‑01, and the fee is Tasa 790‑052.

3. Step‑by‑step: consular vs. in‑country

We outline both procedures in detail. Choose the one that fits your situation.

3.1 Consular application (from abroad)

  • Step 1: Document preparation. Gather all documents listed below. Ensure every foreign public document is apostilled and translated by a sworn translator. Make copies.
  • Step 2: Complete form EX‑18. This is the national visa application form. Download from the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs website.
  • Step 3: Pay the visa fee. Approximately €80 (varies by consulate). Keep the receipt.
  • Step 4: Book an appointment. Visit the website of the Spanish consulate in your jurisdiction. Wait times vary from one week to two months. Some consulates allow postal submissions.
  • Step 5: Attend the interview (if required). Some consulates may ask you to come in person to verify your documents and answer questions about your remote work.
  • Step 6: Wait for resolution. The legal maximum is 20 working days, but it can take up to 45 days. You will be notified by email or post.
  • Step 7: Collect the visa. If approved, you receive a 1‑year visa sticker in your passport. You must enter Spain within three months of issuance.
  • Step 8: Apply for the TIE. Within 30 days of arrival, go to the local police station or immigration office to apply for your Foreigner Identity Card (TIE). This card is proof of your residence and is valid for three years (renewable).

3.2 In‑country application (from within Spain)

  • Step 1: Enter Spain legally. You can enter as a tourist (visa‑free if eligible, or with a Schengen visa). Ensure you get an entry stamp. Do not overstay; you must apply within 90 days of arrival.
  • Step 2: Prepare the application (form EX‑01). The document requirements are identical to the consular route, but you also need to prove your legal entry (passport stamp, boarding passes).
  • Step 3: Pay the Tasa 790‑052. This is the fee for the residence authorisation. The amount is around €15‑20 (check current rates).
  • Step 4: Book an appointment at the immigration office. In Madrid, it’s the UGE (Unidad de Grandes Empresas). In other provinces, it’s the provincial immigration office. Use the “sede electrónica” to get a cita previa.
  • Step 5: Submit your application in person. Bring all documents and the fee receipt. They will give you a stamped receipt (resguardo) confirming submission.
  • Step 6: Wait for resolution. The legal maximum is 3 months. If you haven’t received a response within 3 months, the application is considered approved by “silencio positivo” (positive administrative silence). However, we recommend not relying on silence; follow up.
  • Step 7: Receive the approval letter. Once approved, you’ll be notified. Then you have 30 days to apply for the TIE at the police station.
  • Step 8: Get your TIE. The TIE card will be valid for three years from the date of approval.

5. The Beckham Law: how to pay less tax

One of the most powerful incentives of the Digital Nomad Visa is access to the Special Tax Regime for Impatriates, popularly known as the Beckham Law. If you have not been tax resident in Spain in the last five years, you can opt to be taxed as a non‑resident for up to six years. This means a flat 24% tax on Spanish‑source income up to €600,000 (and 47% above that). Crucially, your foreign‑source income (the majority of your remote work earnings) is generally not taxed in Spain unless you remit it to a Spanish bank account. This can result in massive savings compared to the standard progressive rates (which can reach 47%).

How to apply: You must file a formal request (Form 149) with the Spanish tax agency (AEAT) within six months of starting your economic activity in Spain (or before the end of the first tax year). We recommend including a note with your visa application that you intend to opt for this regime, and then follow up with a tax advisor immediately after arrival.

6. Family reunification: spouse, children, and partners

The visa automatically extends to your family members. They receive the same residence duration and can work or study. The definition of family includes:

  • Spouse (marriage certificate required, apostilled + translated).
  • Registered partner (if your country recognises civil unions equivalent to marriage; otherwise, you may need to register as a pareja de hecho in Spain after arrival).
  • Children under 18, or older children with disabilities (birth certificates, plus proof of dependency).
  • Dependent parents? Unfortunately, parents are not included under this visa, only spouse and children. Parents may qualify for other reunification routes after you become a permanent resident.

Income requirement for family: For the first dependent, you need an additional 75% of the monthly SMI (€992); for each subsequent dependent, some sources say 50% but we always calculate 75% to be safe. So for a couple with two children, total required monthly income = €2,646 + (3 × €992) = €5,622.

7. Renewal and the road to permanent residency

The initial residence permit (TIE) is valid for three years. You can renew it for two‑year periods (renewal application must be submitted within 60 days before expiry or up to 90 days after, with a penalty). To renew, you must prove that you still meet the requirements (income, insurance, etc.) and that you have been compliant with Spanish laws. After five years of continuous legal residence, you can apply for permanent residency (residencia de larga duración). After ten years (and passing a language/culture test), you can apply for Spanish nationality (though be aware that some countries do not allow dual citizenship with Spain).

8. Five mistakes that lead to rejection (and how to avoid them)

1. Income miscalculation

Many applicants use gross income instead of net, or forget to account for the extra amount for family. We always recommend showing net income comfortably above the threshold, with bank statements matching the declared salary. If you have variable income, average the last 12 months and ensure it exceeds the minimum.

2. Apostille & translation errors

Criminal records from some countries need a Hague apostille; others require legalisation. Using a non‑sworn translator is a common reason for rejection. Always hire a translator accredited by the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs. The translation must be signed and sealed.

3. Insurance not meeting the standard

Many submit travel insurance or policies with co‑pays. The law requires "full coverage" without co‑insurance. We recommend policies from Sanitas, Adeslas, or specific expat plans from Cigna. Check that the policy explicitly states "no co‑pays" and covers all risks.

4. No proof of one year remote work

Freelancers sometimes lack a clear history. Keep a portfolio of contracts and invoices covering at least the last 12 months. If you just started freelancing, you may need to show prior employment as proof of experience.

5. Applying too late in Spain

If you enter as a tourist and wait until day 89 to apply, you risk overstaying if there are administrative delays. Apply within the first 30 days. Also, ensure your tourist status is valid – some nationalities require a Schengen visa; don't assume you can enter visa‑free.

9. Frequently asked questions (with lawyer answers)

Can I work for a Spanish company while on this visa?
Yes, but your work for Spanish companies cannot exceed 20% of your total professional activity. If you want to work full‑time for a Spanish company, you need a standard work visa (cuenta ajena).
Do I need to register as autónomo (self‑employed) in Spain?
If you are self‑employed and your clients are abroad, you may not need to register as autónomo immediately, but after becoming tax resident, you must comply with Spanish tax obligations. Many digital nomads register as autónomo to invoice properly and access social security. Consult with a tax advisor to determine the best structure.
What happens if my income drops below the threshold during the validity?
The requirement is checked at the time of application and renewal. During the period, you are not required to maintain the exact income, but if you renew, you must again prove the minimum. If you lose your job, you may switch to another residence category (e.g., job seeker) or leave the country. You should inform immigration authorities if your circumstances change significantly.
Can I travel within the Schengen area with this visa?
Yes, once you have the visa (or the TIE), you can travel freely within the Schengen zone for up to 90 days per 180‑day period. The TIE is a valid residence card that allows border‑free travel. Remember to carry your passport and TIE when travelling.
I have a criminal record from 6 years ago but it was expunged. Do I need to declare it?
You must present a criminal record certificate covering the last 5 years. If the record is older than 5 years and not on the certificate, you don't need to declare it. However, if the certificate shows an expunged record, you may need to explain it with a legal opinion. Each case is different; we recommend a consultation.
Is there a minimum investment or business plan required?
No, unlike the Golden Visa, there is no investment requirement. You just need to prove sufficient income. This makes it one of the most accessible residency options for remote workers.
Can I include my unmarried partner?
Yes, if you have a registered partnership (similar to civil union) that is recognised in Spain, or if you can prove a stable de facto relationship (pareja de hecho) through a registered partnership certificate. Unmarried couples without registration may have difficulty; we recommend registering as a pareja de hecho in Spain after arrival, or marrying. The certificate must be apostilled and translated.
What is the difference between the visa and the TIE?
The visa is a sticker in your passport that allows you to enter Spain and stay for one year. The TIE (Tarjeta de Identidad de Extranjero) is the physical residence card you obtain after arrival, and it is valid for three years. The TIE is the official document proving your legal residence.
How long does the whole process take?
Consular route: 1‑3 months for visa approval, then TIE takes another 1‑2 months after arrival. In‑country route: up to 3 months for authorisation, then TIE 1‑2 months. Plan for at least 4‑5 months from start to having the TIE in hand.

10. Life after approval: what to do in your first 30 days

Once you arrive in Spain with your visa, you must take these steps:

  1. Apply for the TIE. Book a cita previa at the nearest police station (oficina de extranjería) to take fingerprints and submit a photo. You will receive a resguardo and then the card in about 3‑4 weeks.
  2. Register for social security (if you plan to work for Spanish clients). Even if you work only for foreign clients, you may need to register as autónomo depending on your situation. Consult a gestor.
  3. Open a Spanish bank account. This will make life easier for paying rent, utilities, and receiving any Spanish income.
  4. Register on the padrón (municipal register). This is mandatory for all residents and is required for many administrative procedures. Go to your local ayuntamiento with your rental contract and passport.
  5. If you opted for the Beckham Law, file form 149 with the tax agency. Do this within six months.
